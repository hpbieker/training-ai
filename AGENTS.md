# Project Instructions

- When the user asks for analyses or comparisons, answer in chat by default.
- Do not create standalone report files for analyses or comparisons unless the user explicitly asks for a file.
- Treat `data/` as temporary local cache/output. It is ignored by git and can contain downloaded activities, streams, scratch outputs and generated reports when explicitly requested.
- For workout analyses, exclude warm-up and cooldown from interval metrics. The user typically warms up for about 12 minutes and cools down for about 3 minutes. Prefer detecting the actual work segment from the power trace rather than using the full stream: look for the point where power rises into a stable target such as 195 W, 200 W, 205 W or the relevant interval target. There is usually a small step up in watts when the work interval starts.
- When recommending structured indoor ERG workouts, keep the interval prescription simple and flat by default: same watt target for every work interval, no progressive stepping or "lift the last interval" unless the user explicitly asks for that.
- Do not limit training analysis to power and heart rate. The user normally has richer sensor data available, especially for indoor cycling. Check available streams per activity and use the relevant ones when present:
  - Mechanical/load: `watts`, `cadence`, `torque`.
  - Heart/cardiovascular: `heartrate`; derive W/HR and HR drift where useful.
  - Respiratory: `respiration` as breathing rate / BR, `tidal_volume` as VT, and `tidal_volume_min` as minute ventilation / VE. Analyze both averages and drift over the workout/intervals, especially BR drift, VE drift, VT drift, and whether rising VE comes from higher BR or deeper VT.
  - Muscle oxygenation: SmO2 and THb come from the user's Moxy sensor. Analyze min, max and drift over intervals/workout, including SmO2 desaturation, re-oxygenation in recoveries, THb trend/drift, and how local muscle oxygenation changes align with power, HR and respiratory drift. For re-oxygenation in recoveries, quantify both how much SmO2 rises during each recovery and the peak SmO2 reached in that recovery. In cases where Moxy data is absent or clearly unusable, leave it out and note that briefly.
  - Thermal/body: `core_temperature`, `skin_temperature`, `heat_strain_index`.
  - Environment: `temp`, `RuuviTemperature`, `Humidity`, `RuuviHumidity`.
  - Wellness/recovery: `hrv` (rMSSD), `restingHR`, `sleepSecs`, `sleepScore`, plus `weight`, `bodyFat`, `vo2max`, `spO2`, `steps` when populated.
- Xert data, when cached, should be used as the preferred activity-load context rather than Intervals.icu load. Prefer activity-level summary fields such as XSS, low/high/peak XSS, XEP in watts, focus, specificity, numeric difficulty, difficulty rating, freshness/status and the Xert fitness signature. Always include the numeric difficulty when summarizing Xert activity load; the text difficulty rating is useful but too coarse on its own. Intervals.icu load can still be used as secondary context, but readiness and training-load language should prioritize Xert XSS. This helps distinguish high aerobic volume from genuinely costly high-intensity sessions.
- For activity analyses, cache and include Garmin activity details when available (`python3 -B scripts/cache_garmin.py activity <intervals_activity_id>`). Use Garmin/Firstbeat fields as secondary context alongside Xert and Intervals: activity training load, TSS/IF, aerobic and anaerobic training effect, training effect label/message, stamina begin/end/min, performance condition trend, calories and Garmin normalized power. Garmin activity load is useful, but Xert XSS remains the preferred primary load language when both exist.
- Garmin performance condition is typically worse for outdoor rides than indoor rides for this user, likely due to differences in power meter/source, variable terrain, coasting/stops, riding position and outdoor conditions. Do not put much weight on outdoor performance condition by itself. Use it only as a secondary trend signal alongside Xert load, Garmin stamina, HR/power response, RPE and the richer sensor data. Indoor performance condition is more comparable across structured indoor sessions.
- For weather forecasts, use Yr/MET Norway's official Locationforecast API (`api.met.no`) as the default source. Prefer cached data under `data/weather/` when available, and avoid ad hoc weather websites unless the MET/Yr API is unavailable or the user explicitly asks for another source. For outdoor ride recommendations, check weather across the likely route/corridor rather than a single point forecast. Use the user's home/start area around Slemdal as a default anchor when relevant, and add likely destination or exposed-route points such as Ytre Enebakk when the planned ride suggests them.
- Training venue preference: the user generally prefers doing structured intervals indoors because the indoor setup is good and controllable. Long, easy rides are good candidates for outdoors when the weather is genuinely pleasant. The user dislikes riding indoors when it is sunny/nice outside, but if outdoor weather is merely mediocre, indoor riding is preferred over going outside just for the sake of it.
- Garmin Connect data, when cached via `gccli`, should be used as extra readiness context. Prioritize training readiness, HRV status/baseline, Body Battery charge/drain, daily stress, sleep details, resting HR and training status. Treat Garmin's aggregated readiness as a second opinion, not as a replacement for Intervals/Xert load and actual workout sensor response. When Garmin recovery time is available and evaluating a planned future session, project the remaining recovery time forward to the planned session time, assuming no intervening training unless known, rather than judging only the cached value at its timestamp. For this user, a low remaining Garmin recovery time such as roughly 14 hours is not a major warning by itself; if Xert recovery is also comfortably fresh, interpret Garmin and Xert together as supporting training, while still using body feel and leg response to scale the session. If quality is planned the following day, keep the intervening session genuinely easy.
- When refreshing Garmin time-series caches for same-day readiness (`heart_rate`, `stress`, Body Battery, etc.), check how recent the newest returned datapoint actually is. Garmin Connect only has data that has synced from the watch/head unit. If the gap between current local time and the newest cached datapoint is large enough to affect a "can I train now?" decision, say that the Garmin cache is stale and ask the user to sync the watch before relying on those signals.
- Xert training advice, when cached, exposes current Recovery Load, recovery days and workout capacity. Use the returned advice fields directly rather than reimplementing Xert's recovery-days formula locally. Do not use per-activity `summary.progression.rl` as current Recovery Load. Do not use Xert tiredness/status/form category as the main readiness signal; prefer the recovery time fields. Express recovery days as hours when discussing readiness. Positive low recovery hours mean Xert indicates the athlete should wait that long before more work that generates low XSS, which means any activity. Positive high recovery hours mean wait before more work that generates high XSS, i.e. activity over threshold power (TP). Positive peak recovery hours mean wait before more work that generates peak XSS, i.e. activity over TP with special relevance to peak-power work. Negative recovery hours mean the athlete is on the fresh side of the relevant Xert threshold. When evaluating a planned future session, base the recommendation on what the recovery hours will be at the planned session time, projected forward from the advice timestamp, assuming no intervening training unless known. For this user, it is normal to train once Xert is just fresh; `0` to `-2` hours is already enough and there is no need to wait until deeply negative/far into fresh. Interpret Xert workout capacity as how much training can be done now while still being just fresh before the next planned Xert workout, not as a generic independent estimate of how much training the athlete can absorb.
- For Garmin stress, do not rely only on daily average stress. When evaluating whether to train again after a workout, inspect stress after the last workout ended. Continuous orange/high stress after the workout suggests the body is still actively working and should reduce same-day training ambition.
- When evaluating readiness after a previous workout, inspect heart rate after that workout when time-series data is available. Do not base the interpretation on latest HR or average post-workout HR, since those are too sensitive to movement and timing. Use the lowest post-workout HR value and especially the lowest sustained 5-minute post-workout HR average, then compare those with the user's recent restingHR baseline. If the lowest sustained post-workout HR remains continuously elevated and never drops close to baseline, treat that as evidence that the system is still working and reduce training ambition, especially for same-day second sessions or next-morning intensity.
- Before same-day or next-morning training recommendations, refresh volatile caches when possible: Garmin day/recent data for Body Battery/stress/readiness and Xert `training-advice` for current recovery load/days/workout capacity.
- For readiness recommendations, prefer a transparent combination of recent training load plus wellness fields actually present: HRV, resting HR, sleep duration and sleep score. Do not assume Garmin Training Readiness or Body Battery are available through Intervals.icu unless those fields appear in the downloaded wellness data.
